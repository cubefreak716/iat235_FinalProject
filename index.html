What is Fracking?

Hydraulic fracturing, also known as fracking, is the process of injecting a mixture of water, sand, and chemicals at high pressures into shale rock to create fissures to access oil or natural gas.

Fracking has increased oil production in countries around the world, offering stability to gas companies in Canada and the US where regulations are more relaxed. While it is an efficient gas retrieval method, it has also resulted in leakage of fracking waste, damaging nearby ecosystems and causing a rise of pollutants in water systems.

Environmental Effects

Category
Air


Methane: Large amounts of methane, a greenhouse gas that is 80% more harmful than CO₂, is released into the atmosphere from fracking wells.

Crystalline Silica: Communities chronically exposed to crystalline silica, a sand-like substance used in fracking slurry, has are at a greater risk for lung disease.

Organic Compounds: Organic compounds, which are toxic in large concentrations, are released from open-air fracking waste dumps.

CO2: The process of transporting large amounts of water and waste by heavy duty trucks further contributes to the amount of greenhouse gases (CO₂) released into the atmosphere.
Category
Land


Farmland: Leakage of waste into farmland devaste the farming companies, making the soil unsafe for agricultural farming.

Earthquakes: Minor earthquakes are a result of constant hydraulic fracturing.
Category
Water


Drinking Water: Containment of slurry water is often imperfect, causing chemicals to leak into fresh water used for agricultural industries.

Habitats: Waste can also leak into aquatic habitats, exposing wildlife to potential health hazards.

Drought: As the fracking process requires large amounts of water, imported water from external sources oftentimes cause drought in urban areas.

Health Effects

Headaches/Migraines

Residents experience more frequent headaches from chemical and noise pollution.

Pregnancy Risks

Women are subject to an increase of birth defects and premature birth, risking the lives of both the mother and child.

Asthma

Neighbouring communities will notice a rise in asthma diagnosis.

Lung Disease

Communities have reported a rise in lung disease and respiratory illness surround fracking sites.

Sillicosis

Silicosis, a side effect of chronic exposure to airborne silica particles, is a lung disease that increases risk of lung cancer and other serious respiratory diseases.

Cancer

Exposure of radioactive waste, removed during the fracking process, can lead to a number of cancers.

Solutions

As fracking economically benefits many countries in the world, including Canada, banning fracking is not a long term solution to the problem. Instead, there are two ways that fracking companies and the government can improve the issues concerning this industry: improving and managing waste pit designs and enforce stricter reporting standards if a spillage were to happen.

Stricter reporting standards, where all spills must be reported, will bring awareness to the damages that hurts the environment.

Waste Storage



In the cases of major leakages occurring in the past, spill reports often state that the cause is unknown and unresolved. A better management of waste dumps, as well as investigating where and why leaks occur is a likely solution for fracking. Without these answers, future designs for storing fracking waste cannot be improved and chemicals will almost always leak into the environment, affecting the habitats around the area. Investing in better technology and methods for storing fracking waste is one key solution to reducing air, land, and water pollution.
